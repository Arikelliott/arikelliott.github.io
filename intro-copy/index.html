The state of the internet

I’ve heard some people lately point out some interesting observations: the Internet as we know it is kind of dying.

The rise of AI, for all of its coolest uses, is resulting in the constant automatic generation of low-value mush that’s getting dumped onto the internet at an alarming rate.

It’s an impressively simple process: take, for example, a Youtube Short video. With the up-and-coming AI technology we have, anyone can steal some random video clip, and automatically identify to some degree of accuracy what’s happening in it, generate a short script, and then generate an AI-voiceover for it before uploading it. In other words, you can almost completely automate the generation of content, and if you throw enough videos onto Youtube for some people to see it and get you some advertising money, you can make a bit of profit this way. And tons of people are doing it.

This is happening all over the place. Twitter, Youtube, Tiktok, you name it. The mainstream social media sites are getting overrun by this low-effort content, hyper-optimized to be prioritized over more meaningful human-made content, so that it’s steadily becoming all you see. And it’s not even just on the five main social media websites everyone spends all their time on: looking for information with your search engine of choice also often gives you a bunch of blog posts generated by AI on every imaginable topic, usually with terrible quality info.

Also, let’s be real. This was hardly the real start of the internet getting worse.

Many years ago, it was revealed by a former Facebook employee that Facebook had been testing changes to their post recommending algorithms. They were showing people different content and seeing how it affected their moods. They were increasing the amount of content shown by total strangers over content from people you actually know. They were promoting and incentivizing posts that caused drama and disagreement to increase engagement. And they were even promoting and censoring content to manipulate world events and their users’ perception of the truth.

Nowadays, this isn’t even shocking anymore. Every social media site does it. Their goals are to increase profits by getting users to spend as much time as possible on their site, and to manipulate the world to suit their own interests. And for us regular people, the end result is burning up more of our time on online services that feed us lies, divide us, and leave us emotionally exhausted.

Okay enough depressing news, what’s the alternative?

Make your own website! It’s cheap, it’s easy, and it’s free! (Okay it’s at least some of those things usually)

But really. It’s not hard, and there’s a million ways to do it inexpensively or actually for free.

Why?

I recently stumbled on a sort of underground internet community. Their goals are basically to bring back the feel of the early internet. Personal websites and blogs that can be whatever you want, discovery through web surfing, stuff like that. It’s a much more manual process than the force-feeding of content that you get from social media today, and requires some work and a little bit of know-how to actually find interesting things on the internet, but it does seem like a healthier place to be.

Here’s the thing with that movement: I won’t elaborate right now, but if you’re much like me, and if you’re in my target audience you probably are, then you might not care for the views on morality expressed in that overall community.

Build your own website too!

It’s really really not hard, I promise!

If you want something really simple, look into something like Wordpress or Squarespace. Most of these give you a textbox and a bunch of easy drag and drop features to make a website how you want. It takes zero programming of any kind, and there’s absolutely nothing wrong with that.

That said, be a hacker! Build it from scratch! Learn some basic HTML (like at https://www.w3schools.com/, maybe some CSS or even Javascript and PHP! Or maybe some neat tools like Hugo or Jekyll to help automate the process!

What else do you need after that? You need a domain name (the website address you type in to go to a website. Like www.eatpopsicles.com is a domain name, for example), and a place to host your website (somewhere in the world, someone has to have a computer that holds all your website pages and files, and gives them to your browser when you connect to that computer). Both of these tend to cost money, but you can get it pretty cheap or sometimes even free depending on how you do it.

I recommend getting a domain name off of some website that offers them (here’s a decent looking list of some sites that offer it), that way you can pick your website name and you actually own it. It’s usually fairly affordable, I have two right now, one from godaddy.com and one from namecheap.com, and it costs me around $30-40 USD for both.

Then get a server. I can’t recommend stuff as easily because I haven’t paid much attention, but there’s sooo many options, including a lot of cheap or free ones. Especially if you willing to be a totally cool hacker! and put in a little legwork on your end. For example, you can upload your website files with git to Github and make a Github Pages website for free (which I think comes with a domain name too). You can get a free Amazon AWS EC2 instance and install it there. You could even get an old computer and, with the right setup, host it from your own house! (Just be a little careful with that one, there’s a lot of things to watch for)
